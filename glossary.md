# OpenDataWorld Glossary

This glossary defines the canonical vocabulary for OpenDataWorld Foundation. These terms should be used consistently across schemas, ontology, governance, architecture, APIs, documentation, policies, and implementations.

## Purpose

The glossary provides a shared operational language for people, systems, agents, datasets, policies, workflows, and decisions.

A term becomes canonical only when it has:

- A clear definition
- A stable meaning
- A known lifecycle
- A relationship to other terms
- Governance ownership
- Validation rules where applicable

## Core Terms

### Access

The ability of a subject to perform an action on a resource under defined conditions.

Access must be explicit, governed, auditable, and revocable.

### Action

A deliberate operation performed by a human, system, agent, workflow, or organization.

Examples include create, read, update, delete, approve, reject, publish, classify, transform, validate, enrich, score, archive, and revoke.

### Agent

An autonomous or semi-autonomous software actor that can perceive context, reason over objectives, use tools, perform actions, and produce outcomes under governance.

An agent must have identity, scope, permissions, constraints, audit trail, and lifecycle state.

### Agentic Workflow

A governed workflow in which one or more agents perform tasks, use tools, exchange context, and escalate decisions to humans when required.

### API

A defined interface through which systems, agents, or users interact with capabilities, data, or services.

An API is an asset and must be documented, versioned, governed, and monitored.

### Asset

Any identifiable thing that has value, ownership, metadata, lifecycle, and governance.

Examples include datasets, schemas, APIs, models, agents, documents, workflows, policies, dashboards, prompts, tools, and decisions.

### Audit

A verifiable record of what happened, when it happened, who or what caused it, what context existed, and what evidence supports it.

### Canonical

The approved, authoritative, governed representation of a concept, schema, policy, entity, or relationship.

Canonical does not mean permanent. It means governed, versioned, and treated as the current source of truth until superseded.

### Classification

The process of assigning one or more categories, labels, sensitivity levels, domains, or taxonomic values to an asset or entity.

### Consent

A recorded permission granted by a person, organization, or authorized representative for a defined purpose, scope, duration, and processing activity.

Consent must be traceable, revocable, and auditable.

### Context

The set of relevant facts, constraints, history, relationships, permissions, environment, and intent required to interpret or act correctly.

### Data Asset

A governed asset that contains, references, describes, produces, or transforms data.

Examples include datasets, tables, files, APIs, streams, reports, embeddings, indexes, and knowledge graph records.

### Dataset

A structured or semi-structured collection of data published, governed, versioned, and described with metadata.

A dataset must have ownership, provenance, quality indicators, access policy, license, lifecycle state, and schema where applicable.

### Data Product

A data asset packaged for consumption with clear ownership, quality expectations, documentation, access model, usage terms, and service-level expectations.

### Data Source

An origin system, file, stream, database, API, sensor, application, or external provider from which data is collected or referenced.

### Decision

A selected course of action or judgment made by a human, system, agent, or organization based on context, evidence, constraints, and objectives.

A decision must be traceable when it affects governance, access, risk, quality, compliance, or user outcomes.

### Decision Intelligence

The discipline and capability of improving decisions through data, context, models, reasoning, evidence, governance, feedback, and measurable outcomes.

### Entity

A uniquely identifiable person, organization, place, thing, concept, asset, system, event, action, or relationship.

Entities must have stable identifiers and governed metadata.

### Event

A time-bound record that something happened.

Events are append-only observations and may cause state changes, workflows, alerts, decisions, or audits.

### Evidence

Information used to support a claim, decision, classification, score, policy outcome, or audit finding.

Evidence must preserve source, timestamp, confidence, and provenance.

### FAIR

A set of principles that make data Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable.

OpenDataWorld uses FAIR as a guiding model for data assets and data products.

### Governance

The system of rules, roles, policies, processes, controls, and accountability mechanisms that guide how assets are created, accessed, used, changed, shared, archived, and deleted.

### Human Approval

An explicit decision by an authorized human to approve, reject, override, escalate, or modify a proposed action or outcome.

Human approval is required where policy, risk, law, ethics, financial impact, or operational criticality demands it.

### Identity

A persistent representation of a person, organization, system, agent, service, or asset.

Identity enables authentication, authorization, accountability, trust, lifecycle management, and provenance.

### Interoperability

The ability of different systems, organizations, tools, agents, and data models to exchange and correctly interpret information through shared standards and protocols.

### Knowledge Asset

A governed asset that represents reusable knowledge.

Examples include documents, taxonomies, ontologies, embeddings, prompts, playbooks, policies, research notes, decision logs, and knowledge graph fragments.

### Knowledge Graph

A graph-based representation of entities, relationships, attributes, provenance, and context.

A knowledge graph connects data, knowledge, people, systems, agents, actions, decisions, and policies.

### Lifecycle

The governed set of states through which an asset, entity, policy, dataset, agent, or workflow passes from creation to retirement.

Typical states include draft, proposed, active, deprecated, archived, suspended, revoked, and deleted.

### Lineage

The traceable history of how data or an asset was created, transformed, moved, derived, consumed, or published.

Lineage must connect sources, transformations, actors, timestamps, and outputs.

### Metadata

Data that describes an asset, entity, process, relationship, or event.

Metadata may include title, description, owner, schema, tags, license, version, provenance, classification, quality, lifecycle state, and access policy.

### Model

A formal or computational representation used for reasoning, prediction, classification, generation, validation, scoring, or decision support.

A model is an asset and must be versioned, evaluated, governed, and monitored.

### Ontology

A formal representation of concepts, entity types, relationships, constraints, and meanings within a domain.

The ontology defines what things exist and how they relate.

### Open Data

Data that can be accessed, used, modified, and shared under clearly defined terms.

Open data still requires governance, provenance, quality controls, licensing, and responsible use.

### Outcome

The result produced by an action, workflow, decision, policy, agent, or system.

Outcomes should be measurable where possible.

### Policy

A rule or set of rules that determines what is allowed, denied, required, recommended, or escalated under defined conditions.

Policies must be versioned, testable, auditable, and enforceable where possible.

### Provenance

The origin and history of an asset, entity, claim, dataset, decision, or result.

Provenance records where something came from, how it changed, who or what changed it, and what evidence supports it.

### Quality

The degree to which an asset is fit for its intended purpose.

Quality may include accuracy, completeness, consistency, timeliness, validity, uniqueness, reliability, and usability.

### Registry

A governed catalog of canonical records for assets, entities, schemas, policies, agents, datasets, models, tools, or other managed objects.

### Relationship

A meaningful connection between two or more entities.

Relationships must have type, direction where applicable, context, provenance, and lifecycle state.

### Resource

Anything that can be accessed, used, governed, protected, or referenced.

A resource may be a dataset, API, file, service, system, model, tool, schema, policy, or knowledge object.

### Schema

A formal definition of the structure, fields, constraints, types, and validation rules for data or metadata.

Schemas must be versioned and governed.

### Schema Registry

A governed registry that stores, versions, validates, and publishes schemas.

### Standard

An agreed specification, rule, protocol, vocabulary, or practice used to enable interoperability, quality, governance, or compliance.

### Subject

An actor that requests or performs access or action.

Examples include people, organizations, systems, services, agents, and workflows.

### Taxonomy

A governed classification system that organizes concepts, entities, assets, or domains into categories.

A taxonomy defines how things are grouped.

### Tool

A capability that can be invoked by a human, system, agent, or workflow to perform an action.

Tools must have identity, interface, permissions, constraints, risk rating, and audit trail.

### Traceability

The ability to follow the path of an asset, action, decision, event, or outcome across time, systems, actors, and transformations.

### Trust

The measurable confidence that an asset, entity, system, agent, source, decision, or process is reliable, governed, authentic, and fit for use.

Trust should be evidence-based, contextual, and continuously reassessed.

### Validation

The process of checking whether data, metadata, schema, policy, action, or outcome conforms to defined rules and expectations.

### Version

A specific, identifiable state of an asset, schema, policy, model, dataset, document, or decision record.

Versions must be traceable and should not be overwritten without governance.

### Workflow

A defined sequence or graph of actions, decisions, events, actors, tools, and controls that produces an outcome.

Workflows must be observable, auditable, and governable.

## Governance Rules for Glossary Terms

1. Every canonical term must have a stable definition.
2. Every term should map to related ontology entities where applicable.
3. Every term used in schemas must appear in this glossary or an approved domain glossary.
4. Ambiguous terms must be clarified before being used in contracts, schemas, APIs, or policies.
5. Deprecated terms must remain documented with replacement terms and migration guidance.
6. Terms should prefer open standards vocabulary where a suitable standard exists.
